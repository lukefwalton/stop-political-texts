# FAQ: Stop Political Spam Texts

Short answers to the things people ask most about this privacy-first iOS SMS
filter for political campaign texts. These mirror the in-app FAQ.

### Why is it $0.99?

Because Apple charges $100 a year to publish apps. The 99 cents helps cover
that, nothing more. You pay once, and there is no data business behind it.

### Do you see my texts?

No. Filtering runs entirely on your device. Message content and sender
information are never sent to a server and are never stored by the app.

### Is there an account or login?

None. No account, no login, no analytics, no tracking, no ads. There is nothing
to sign up for.

### Does it block texts from people I know?

No. iOS only routes texts from unknown senders to filters like this one.
Messages from your contacts stay in your inbox.

### Where do filtered texts go?

To the Junk folder under Unknown Senders in Messages. They are still there if you
ever want to read them.

### Does it favor a political party?

No. The app does not distinguish between parties, candidates, or ideologies.
Every political campaign category is filtered equally.

### Can I turn it off?

Anytime. Flip the toggle in the app, or remove it as your SMS filter in iOS
Settings.

### Why do I have to set it up in Settings?

Apple requires you to choose an SMS filter yourself in Settings, and iOS will
not let an app open that exact screen. So there is one short trip to Settings,
then you are done.

### How is this different from Bouncer or other SMS filters?

General-purpose filters like Bouncer and Veto give you allow and block word
lists to catch any unwanted SMS, which means you write and maintain the rules
yourself. Stop Political Spam Texts is focused on one job: it ships with a
maintained classifier tuned for political campaign, fundraising, polling,
ballot-measure, PAC, party, and election texts, so political detection works out
of the box. You can still add your own custom blocked or allowed terms. Both
approaches are privacy-friendly and local-first, but this app is narrower by
design, and credits Bouncer and Veto as design inspiration (see the README
acknowledgements).

### Is the source code public?

Yes. The full source is published so you can verify every privacy promise for
yourself. It is not open source — see the license in the repository.
